Speaker Notes: These four pillars are what separate a developer who uses AI effectively from one who is replaced by AI. Walk through each: (1) Curiosity — the tools change every month. The people who thrive are the ones who try the new thing on day one, not the ones who wait until it's mandatory. (2) Fundamentals — you can't direct a tool you don't understand. If you don't know how HTTP works, you can't tell the AI to "use a POST request with a JSON body" vs. "submit the form." (3) Communication — the better you write, the better AI works for you. Pillar 1: Be Relentlessly Curious

The Learning Never Stops

"The illiterate of the 21st century will not be those who cannot read and write, but those who cannot learn, unlearn, and relearn."
— Alvin Toffler

Practical Curiosity

  • • Try a new AI tool every month
  • • Read changelogs of tools you use
  • • Follow developers who build these tools
  • • Build a small project with each new tool
  • • Share what you learn with peers

The Unlearning Part

  • • "That's how we've always done it" is dangerous
  • • Best practices evolve — stay updated
  • • What was true 2 years ago may not be true today
  • • Be willing to change your workflow
  • • Don't let ego block better approaches
Speaker Notes: The Toffler quote is from 1970 but feels more relevant than ever. The rate of change in AI tools is unprecedented. Claude Code didn't exist a year ago. Cursor was a niche tool 18 months ago. Students who graduate and stop learning will be outdated within a year. But curiosity isn't just about AI — it's about understanding your domain, your users, adjacent technologies. The "unlearning" part is equally important: if you learned that "you should never let AI write code for you," that was good advice in 2022 but limiting advice in 2026.
F

Pillar 2: Know the Fundamentals

You Can't Direct What You Don't Understand

A developer who knows fundamentals + AI tools will always outperform one who knows only AI tools.

What to Know Deeply

  • • How HTTP requests and responses work
  • • How the DOM renders and updates
  • • JavaScript fundamentals (scope, closures, async)
  • • State management patterns
  • • Web security basics (XSS, CORS, auth)
  • • How to read and debug code

Why Fundamentals + AI = Superpower

  • • You can write precise specs (you know the vocabulary)
  • • You catch AI mistakes (you know what's wrong)
  • • You debug faster (you understand the stack)
  • • You make architecture decisions (AI can't)
  • • You know when AI is hallucinating
  • • You can learn new frameworks quickly

Everything we've covered in this course — HTML, CSS, JS, HTTP, React, security — is more valuable now, not less.

Speaker Notes: This is the most important message for students who might be wondering "why am I learning JavaScript if AI can write it for me?" The answer: BECAUSE AI can write it, you need to understand it even more. You're the quality control. You're the architect. You're the one who decides whether the AI's output is good or dangerous. Connect to the course: "Everything we've learned — HTTP in Week 4, React in Week 5, security in Week 9 — these are the fundamentals that let you use AI tools effectively. Without them, you're just accepting code you don't understand."
A

Pillar 3: Write and Communicate Clearly

To People AND to Machines

Writing for AI

  • • Precise specifications get precise output
  • • Ambiguity in your prompt = ambiguity in the result
  • • Structure your requests (context, task, constraints)
  • • Use examples to anchor expectations
  • • State what you DON'T want as clearly as what you do

Writing for People

  • • Clear commit messages explain WHY, not just what
  • • Good PR descriptions save reviewer time
  • • Documentation helps future-you (and your team)
  • • Bug reports with reproduction steps get fixed faster
  • • Emails that get to the point get responses
"Clear thinking becomes clear writing; one can't exist without the other."
— William Zinsser, On Writing Well
Speaker Notes: Here's the irony: in an age of AI, the most valuable skill might be WRITING. Not writing code — writing in human language. The better you can express what you want, the better AI tools work for you. But it's the same skill that makes you effective with people: writing clear specs, clear commit messages, clear documentation. Ask students: "How many of you have spent 30 minutes trying to understand a vague Slack message or email?" Clear writing is a superpower in any profession, and it's even more important now. Point out that the spec-driven process from Part 3 is fundamentally a writing exercise.
!

Pillar 4: Find the Real Problem

Build What Matters

The most dangerous trap: building a perfect solution to the wrong problem.

Ask "Why?" Five Times

  • Request: "Build a dashboard"
  • Why? "To see sales data"
  • Why? "To know which products sell"
  • Why? "To decide what to restock"
  • Why? "To avoid running out of popular items"
  • Real need: Automated restock alerts, not a dashboard

Problem-Finding Skills

  • • Talk to actual users, not just stakeholders
  • • Watch people use existing solutions
  • • Ask "what's the most painful part of your day?"
  • • Look for workarounds — they reveal real needs
  • • Question every assumption
  • • An unused feature is worse than a missing one

AI makes it easy to build anything. Your job is to figure out what's worth building.

Speaker Notes: This is the highest-leverage skill. AI dramatically reduces the cost of building things. When building is cheap, the bottleneck shifts to knowing WHAT to build. Walk through the "5 Whys" example — the initial request was "build a dashboard" but the real need was automated alerts. The dashboard would have taken weeks to build and might have gone unused. The alert system takes a day and solves the actual problem. This is where human judgment is irreplaceable. AI can build either solution, but it can't tell you which one matters. This is what makes software engineering a creative profession, not just a technical one.

The Developer's Evolving Role

From Typist to Director

Yesterday's Developer

  • • Writes every line of code manually
  • • Memorizes syntax and APIs
  • • Measures productivity by lines of code
  • • Works alone in a text editor
  • • Valued for speed of typing

Tomorrow's Developer

  • • Directs AI agents to implement solutions
  • • Understands systems and architecture
  • • Measures productivity by problems solved
  • • Orchestrates human + AI collaboration
  • • Valued for judgment and taste
"The best developers won't be the ones who write the most code. They'll be the ones who solve the most important problems."
— The central thesis of this lecture
Speaker Notes: This is the capstone message. The role isn't disappearing — it's evolving. Think of it like filmmaking: a director doesn't hold the camera, light the set, or edit the footage. They make the creative decisions about what story to tell and how to tell it. A film with great technicians but no director produces nothing memorable. A director with a clear vision and skilled team (including AI "team members") can create something remarkable. Students shouldn't feel threatened by AI — they should feel empowered. The bar for what one person can build has been raised dramatically.
